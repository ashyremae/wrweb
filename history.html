History

The arrival of the Windrush generation in the UK in 1948 marked a turning point in British history, as Caribbean migrants arrived to support post-war labour shortages. However, their children were soon caught in a system of racial discrimination, particularly within education. Many were misdiagnosed as ‘educationally subnormal’ and wrongly placed into ESN schools.

Bernard Coard’s 1971 pamphlet, How the West Indian Child is Made Educationally Subnormal in the British School System, exposed the systemic bias that labelled Black children as unintelligent. He argued that this misrepresentation was not accidental but structurally embedded in British schooling.

This injustice is deeply personal to me. My father, a second-generation Windrush descendant, was diagnosed with dyslexia but failed by a system that viewed his differences through a racial lens. My great-grandparents came to Britain for opportunity, but my father's schooling experience tells a different story—one of marginalisation and low expectations.
